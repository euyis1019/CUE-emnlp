We organize the material into four parts: aggregate outcome fingerprints with per-task commentary (\cref{app:subsec:outcome_fingerprints_full}), a detailed sweep over the Computing task's difficulty axes (\cref{app:subsec:computing_density_sweep}), per-difficulty-level breakdowns for all six tasks (\cref{app:subsec:per_difficulty_breakdown}), and brewing dynamics as a function of difficulty (\cref{app:subsec:brewing_dynamics_comparison}).

\textbf{Key observation.}
Computing is the only task family where all four outcomes appear in substantial proportions ($>$15\% each for Res., OT, and MR+UR combined).
The remaining five tasks share a common signature: Resolved $>$ 79\%, Overprocessed as the primary failure mode (8--21\%), and Misresolved and Unresolved together below 6\%.
This dichotomy---\emph{arithmetic-heavy vs.\ control-flow}---is the organizing principle for the analyses that follow.

\subsection{Computing Density Sweep}
\label{app:subsec:computing_density_sweep}

% 中文: Computing 任务有两个正交的难度轴——ρ (compute density) 和 steps (chain length)。
% 本节分别做 sweep，揭示二者对 outcome 分布和 brewing dynamics 的不同影响。

The Computing task has two orthogonal difficulty axes: the compute density $\rho \in \{0, 0.5, 1.0\}$, which controls the fraction of chain steps requiring arithmetic (vs.\ pure variable lookup), and the chain length (\emph{steps} $\in \{2,3,4,5\}$), which determines the number of sequential reasoning hops.
We sweep each axis independently to isolate their effects.

\textbf{Analysis.}
Even at $\rho{=}0$, the Resolved rate is notably lower than that of structurally similar control-flow tasks, confirming that \textbf{multi-hop variable tracking itself constitutes a bottleneck}---arithmetic is not the sole source of difficulty.
As $\rho$ increases, the Resolved rate drops further, but the \emph{character} of failure shifts: Overprocessed remains the dominant failure mode at $\rho{=}0$, whereas at $\rho{=}1.0$, Misresolved and Unresolved grow disproportionately.
This indicates that arithmetic demands do not simply make the task harder in a uniform sense; they \textbf{change the failure mechanism} from late-layer corruption (OT) to the model's inability to reach the correct answer at all (MR/UR).
The mean brewing duration $\overline{\Delta}_{\mathrm{brew}}$ also contracts with increasing $\rho$, consistent with the probe detecting partial (but incorrect) information earlier when arithmetic noise contaminates the representation.

\textbf{Analysis.}
Increasing chain length degrades outcomes \emph{even within fixed $\rho$ strata}, demonstrating that sequential depth is an independent difficulty factor.
At steps$\,{=}\,2$, the task resembles a simple lookup and achieves comparatively high Resolved rates; by steps$\,{=}\,5$, performance deteriorates substantially.
Notably, FPCL shifts later with longer chains---the probe requires more layers to detect even partial correctness---while FJC remains relatively stable, compressing the brewing window $\Delta_{\mathrm{brew}}$.
This compression mirrors the $\rho$-sweep pattern: harder instances leave less room for the representation to mature before the model must commit.

\textbf{Key findings.}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Copying} maintains high Resolved rates across all step counts, with Overprocessed as the sole failure mode. The absence of MR and UR at every difficulty level confirms that pure variable lookup never fails to converge---when errors occur, they are exclusively due to late-layer corruption.

\item \textbf{Computing ($\rho{=}1.0$)} shows a steep decline in Resolved rate as steps increase. At steps$\,{=}\,2$, the profile is OT-dominated; by steps$\,{=}\,5$, MR and UR together can exceed OT, indicating that the model increasingly fails to reach the correct answer at all rather than merely losing it in late layers.

\item \textbf{Conditional} is notable for its \textbf{depth-dependent Misresolved emergence}: at depth 1, MR is near zero, but at depth 3, MR rises substantially. This suggests that deeply nested conditional branches introduce genuine ambiguity in the model's internal resolution process, causing convergence to an incorrect answer rather than simply failing to converge.

\item \textbf{Short-circuit} maintains \textbf{zero Unresolved across all difficulty levels}, even at num\_conditions$\,{=}\,3$. The model always commits to an answer for short-circuit evaluation, consistent with the binary (early-exit) nature of the task. Failures partition cleanly into OT and MR.

\item \textbf{Loop vs.\ Loop Unrolled}: at the aggregate level, these two tasks have similar outcome distributions. However, the per-difficulty breakdown reveals that the \textbf{degradation curves differ in shape}: Loop shows a more gradual decline with iteration count, while Loop Unrolled degrades more steeply at iteration$\,{=}\,4$. This suggests that syntactic loop structure provides a processing scaffold that partially buffers against iteration-count difficulty, whereas unrolled code lacks this scaffold and becomes harder to track at high iteration counts.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Analysis.}
Three patterns emerge from \cref{tab:app:brewing_per_difficulty}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Positional stability of the brewing scaffold.}
Across all tasks, the mean FPCL and FJC positions do not shift dramatically with difficulty.
For instance, in the Loop task, FPCL remains in the range of layer 4--6 and FJC in the range of layer 19--22 regardless of iteration count.
This stability indicates that the model's \emph{processing architecture}---the layers responsible for information encoding (FPCL) and commitment (FJC)---is largely fixed and task-difficulty-independent.

\item \textbf{FJC existence rate as the primary difficulty-sensitive variable.}
What changes with difficulty is not \emph{where} the model processes information, but \emph{whether} it successfully reaches a convergence point at all.
As difficulty increases, FJC existence rate declines, and the ``missing'' samples fall into the Misresolved or Unresolved categories.
This cleanly separates the model's \emph{mechanism} (stable) from its \emph{capability} (difficulty-dependent).

\item \textbf{Brewing duration compression under difficulty.}
In tasks where FPCL shifts later with difficulty (notably Computing), $\Delta_{\mathrm{brew}}$ compresses because FJC remains anchored.
A shorter brewing window leaves less representational ``room'' for the correct answer to mature, which may partly explain the increased failure rates at high difficulty.
\end{enumerate}

These findings reinforce the central narrative of the paper: the model deploys a \textbf{consistent internal processing scaffold} across tasks and difficulty levels, but the \textbf{capacity of that scaffold} is bounded.
When task demands exceed this capacity, the outcome shifts from Resolved to one of the three failure modes---which specific failure mode occurs is determined by the task's computational character (arithmetic vs.\ control flow), not by the scaffold's structure.
