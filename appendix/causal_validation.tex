\section{Causal Validation Experiments}
\label{app:causal_validation_details}

\subsection{Activation Patching at FJC}
\label{app:subsec:activation_patching_fjc}

We construct $N=150$ task-matched sample pairs from the anchor setting (Qwen2.5-Coder-7B).
Each pair shares the same task family but differs in ground-truth answer.
Patching replaces the last-token hidden state $\mathbf{h}^\ell$ at layer $\ell$ with that of the paired sample; the model then continues from layer $\ell+1$.
We measure the \emph{answer-flip rate}: the fraction of samples whose final prediction changes to the paired sample's answer.

A layer-wise sweep reveals a clear peak around the mean FJC position.
The flip rate at the FJC layer reaches \textbf{70.0\%}, compared with \textbf{18.7\%} at pre-brewing layers (layer 17) and \textbf{30.7\%} at post-resolution layers (layer 27), confirming that FJC serves as a causally privileged bottleneck for answer-relevant information.

\subsection{Layer Skipping for Overprocessed}
\label{app:subsec:layer_skipping_overprocessed}

We skip all layers after FJC by decoding directly from $\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{FJC}}$ via LayerNorm and the unembedding matrix, bypassing the tail layers hypothesized to destroy correct computations in Overprocessed samples.

The intervention is sharply outcome-specific (\cref{tab:app:layer_skipping}): 73.3\% of Overprocessed samples are rescued, Resolved samples suffer no degradation, and the modest 20\% Misresolved recovery is expected given the absence of a joint-correct layer.

\subsection{Late-Layer Hidden-State Sparsity in Overprocessed Samples}
\label{app:subsec:late_layer_sparsity_overprocessed}

As a descriptive complement to the layer-skipping intervention, we examine whether Overprocessed samples exhibit distinct late-layer representation geometry.
Using the full Qwen2.5-Coder-7B Computing dataset ($N=1800$), we measure two sparsity indices---Gini and Hoyer---from the last-token hidden state at every layer.

\cref{fig:app:hidden_state_sparsity} shows that Gini peaks in middle-to-late layers while Hoyer spikes near layer 26, consistent with a two-stage picture of concentration followed by sparsification.
Comparing Overprocessed and non-Overprocessed samples at layers 24--27 (\cref{fig:app:hoyer_ot_vs_nonot}), we find consistently higher Hoyer sparsity for Overprocessed samples, with the largest gap at layer 26.
As this analysis is observational, we treat sparsity as a representation-level correlate rather than causal evidence of overprocessing.

\subsection{Re-injection for Unresolved}
\label{app:subsec:reinjection_unresolved}

We test whether Unresolved samples contain partially usable computations by providing additional effective depth.
The hidden state $\mathbf{h}^{L-1}$ from layer $L-1$ is injected into a fresh forward pass at layer $\ell_{\mathrm{inject}}$; this can be repeated $k$ times.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Re-injection rescue rates for Unresolved samples ($N=150$). $k$ denotes the number of re-injection rounds. The Resolved control group maintains 100\% accuracy throughout.}
\label{tab:app:reinjection}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
$k$ & Rescue rate & Resolved control acc. \\
\midrule
1     & \textbf{33.3\%} & 100\% \\
2     & \textbf{40.0\%} & 100\% \\
3     & \textbf{46.7\%} & 100\% \\
Never & 53.3\% remain unrescued & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Three rounds of re-injection rescue 46.7\% of Unresolved samples (\cref{tab:app:reinjection}), while the Resolved control maintains perfect accuracy.
This indicates that nearly half of Unresolved samples are ``not finished'' rather than ``fundamentally incapable.''
The remaining 53.3\% likely represent cases at the model's capability boundary.

\subsection{Cross-Task Patching Sensitivity Profile}
\label{app:subsec:cross_task_patching}

We extend activation patching to four tasks---Copying, Computing, Conditional, and Loop---on Qwen2.5-Coder-7B to test whether the FJC bottleneck generalises across task families.

\paragraph{Setup.}
For each task we sample $N{=}150$ task-matched pairs and sweep the patching layer across an 11-layer window centred on the per-task mean FJC ($\mathrm{FJC}{-}5$ to $\mathrm{FJC}{+}5$).

\paragraph{Analysis.}
\cref{tab:app:cross_task_patching} and \cref{fig:app:cross_task_patching} reveal two patterns.
First, all four tasks show a clear flip-rate peak at FJC, confirming that the causal bottleneck generalises beyond the anchor setting.
Second, \emph{peak sharpness} differs systematically: Copying exhibits the narrowest window (answer crystallises abruptly once variable binding resolves), while Conditional and Loop show broader windows (answer depends on multi-layer evaluation of branching or iteration), with Computing in between.
This mirrors the distinction between stabilisation and commitment bottlenecks discussed in \S4, providing causal evidence that different code primitives induce structurally distinct processing patterns.

\subsection{Failure Cases}
\label{app:subsec:causal_failure_cases}

\paragraph{Patching failures.}
Approximately 30\% of samples show low flip rates at FJC, likely because answer information is distributed across multiple layers or encoded in dimensions not captured by last-token patching.

\paragraph{Layer-skipping failures.}
Among Overprocessed samples, 26.7\% remain incorrect after skipping, suggesting that degradation may begin before or at FJC in some cases.

\paragraph{Re-injection failures.}
The 53.3\% never-rescued Unresolved samples likely represent genuine capability gaps rather than insufficient depth.

\paragraph{Summary.}
These failure modes confirm that our taxonomy captures dominant patterns but is not a perfect partition---some samples resist clean categorization. We view this transparency about limitations as strengthening the framework's credibility.
