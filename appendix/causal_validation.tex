\section{Causal Validation Experiments}
\label{app:causal_validation_details}

% 本节提供正文 §3.1 三组因果实验的完整设置、层位扫描结果与失败案例。

\subsection{Activation Patching at FJC}
\label{app:subsec:activation_patching_fjc}

% 详细实验设置：task-matched sample 配对策略、patching 操作流程。
% 层位扫描：不同层的 answer-flip rate 曲线，展示 FJC 的因果特权性。

We construct $N=150$ task-matched sample pairs from the anchor setting (Qwen2.5-Coder-7B).
Each pair shares the same task family but has a different ground-truth answer.
The patching operation replaces the last-token hidden state $\mathbf{h}^\ell$ at target layer $\ell$ with the corresponding hidden state from the paired sample, and the model continues its forward pass from layer $\ell+1$ onward.
We measure the \emph{answer-flip rate}: the fraction of samples whose final prediction changes to the paired sample's answer after patching.

A layer-wise sweep reveals a clear peak around the mean FJC position.
The flip rate at the FJC layer reaches \textbf{69.9\%}, compared with \textbf{18.7\%} at matched pre-brewing layers (layer 17) and \textbf{31.0\%} at post-resolution layers (layer 27).
The elevated flip rate at FJC confirms that this layer serves as a causally privileged bottleneck: it concentrates the answer-relevant information that determines the model's final prediction.

\subsection{Layer Skipping for Overprocessed}
\label{app:subsec:layer_skipping_overprocessed}

% 跳过 FJC 之后尾部层的具体操作与解码方式。
% Resolved vs. Overprocessed 上的效果对比，展示 outcome 特异性。

We skip all layers after FJC by taking $\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{FJC}}$ and directly applying LayerNorm followed by the unembedding matrix to decode the prediction.
This bypasses the tail layers that, in the Overprocessed case, are hypothesized to destroy a correct computation.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Layer-skipping results by outcome group. Pre-skip accuracy is the standard model accuracy; post-skip accuracy is obtained by decoding from $\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{FJC}}$.}
\label{tab:app:layer_skipping}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Outcome group & $N$ & Pre-skip acc. & Post-skip acc. \\
\midrule
Overprocessed       & 75  & 0.0\%   & \textbf{73.3\%} \\
Resolved (control)  & 25  & 100.0\% & \textbf{100.0\%} \\
Misresolved         & 50  & 0.0\%   & 20.0\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The results (\cref{tab:app:layer_skipping}) confirm that the intervention is sharply outcome-specific: 73.3\% of Overprocessed samples are rescued by skipping the tail layers, while already-Resolved samples suffer no degradation whatsoever.
The modest 20\% recovery among Misresolved samples is expected, as these lack a joint-correct layer by definition.

\subsection{Late-Layer Hidden-State Sparsity in Overprocessed Samples}
\label{app:subsec:late_layer_sparsity_overprocessed}

As a descriptive complement to the layer-skipping intervention, we also examine whether Overprocessed samples exhibit a distinct late-layer representation geometry.
Using the full Qwen2.5-Coder-7B Computing dataset ($N=1800$), we aggregate two standard sparsity measures from the last-token hidden state at every layer: the Gini index and Hoyer sparsity.

\cref{fig:app:hidden_state_sparsity} shows that the two measures peak at different depths.
The Gini index rises earlier and reaches its maximum around the middle-to-late layers, whereas Hoyer sparsity stays relatively moderate until the final block and then spikes sharply near layer 26.
This temporal separation is consistent with a two-stage picture in which representations first become more concentrated and later undergo a stronger sparsification during the final decoding-relevant phase.

To test whether this late sparsification is specifically associated with degradation after internal success, we compare Overprocessed and non-Overprocessed valid samples at layers 24--27.
As shown in \cref{fig:app:hoyer_ot_vs_nonot}, Overprocessed samples have consistently higher Hoyer sparsity at all four late layers, with the largest gap at layer 26 where global Hoyer also peaks.
Because this analysis is observational rather than interventional, we do not treat sparsity as causal evidence.
Instead, we view it as a representation-level correlate of overprocessing: once a correct answer has become jointly available, the final degradation phase is accompanied by a measurable compression-like shift in hidden-state geometry.

\subsection{Re-injection for Unresolved}
\label{app:subsec:reinjection_unresolved}

% 重注入流程：如何将后期 hidden state 注入新的前向传播以提供额外有效深度。

We test whether Unresolved samples contain incomplete but partially usable computations by providing the model with additional effective depth via re-injection.
Specifically, we take the hidden state $\mathbf{h}^{L-1}$ from layer $L-1$ of the original forward pass and inject it into a fresh forward pass at layer $\ell_{\mathrm{inject}}$, allowing the model to continue processing from that point onward.
This procedure can be repeated $k$ times to provide progressively more effective depth.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Re-injection rescue rates for Unresolved samples ($N=150$). $k$ denotes the number of re-injection rounds. The Resolved control group maintains 100\% accuracy throughout.}
\label{tab:app:reinjection}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
$k$ & Rescue rate & Resolved control acc. \\
\midrule
1     & \textbf{33.3\%} & 100\% \\
2     & \textbf{40.0\%} & 100\% \\
3     & \textbf{46.7\%} & 100\% \\
Never & 53.3\% remain unrescued & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

As shown in \cref{tab:app:reinjection}, a single re-injection already rescues 33.3\% of Unresolved samples, and three rounds of re-injection rescue 46.7\%.
The Resolved control group maintains perfect accuracy, confirming that the procedure does not introduce artifacts.
These results indicate that nearly half of Unresolved samples contain computation that is incomplete but already on a productive trajectory---they are ``not finished'' rather than ``fundamentally incapable.''
The remaining 53.3\% that are never rescued likely represent cases where the model lacks the requisite capability for the given input.

\subsection{Failure Cases}
\label{app:subsec:causal_failure_cases}

% 三组因果实验中干预无效的典型案例分析。

Each causal experiment has a non-trivial failure rate, and analyzing these failures is important for understanding the boundaries of our taxonomy.

\paragraph{Patching failures.}
Approximately 30\% of samples show a low flip rate even when patching at FJC.
This may occur because FJC is not the sole causally relevant layer for these samples---answer information may be distributed across multiple layers or encoded in dimensions not fully captured by last-token patching.

\paragraph{Layer-skipping failures.}
Among Overprocessed samples, 26.7\% remain incorrect after skipping the tail layers.
In these cases, the hidden state at FJC may already have begun to degrade, or the correct answer may require additional post-FJC processing that our skipping procedure eliminates.

\paragraph{Re-injection failures.}
The 53.3\% of Unresolved samples that are never rescued, even after three re-injection rounds, likely represent instances where the model fundamentally lacks the capability to solve the given input, rather than merely needing more depth.
These cases suggest a ``capability boundary'' that additional depth alone cannot overcome.

\paragraph{Summary.}
These failure modes confirm that our taxonomy, while capturing the dominant patterns, is not a perfect partition of all computational trajectories.
The boundaries between outcome categories are not always sharp, and some samples may resist clean categorization.
We view this transparency about method limitations as strengthening rather than undermining the framework's credibility.
